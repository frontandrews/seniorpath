## The problem

Technical communication often fails at one of two extremes.

Either the person says too little and leaves gaps in the reasoning.

Or they say too much and force the other side to separate signal from noise.

## Mental model

Communicating well is not about impressing people.

It is about reducing the interpretation work for the person listening.

The useful question is:

> After I say this, will the other person understand the state, the decision, and the next step?

If the answer is no, the explanation is still weak.

## Breaking it down

A simple way to communicate more clearly is:

1. lead with the main point
2. add only the context that changes the decision
3. name the risk or trade-off when it matters
4. close with the next step or the clear position

That works in standups, design discussions, and interviews.

## Simple example

Compare these two answers:

> I touched several parts and I am still checking a few things.

and

> Yesterday I finished the main integration. Today I am fixing the last errors. The only real risk is the external API still being unstable.

The second answer is better not because it is formal, but because it says what matters.

## Common mistakes

- starting with detail before the main point
- assuming context alone creates clarity
- hiding risk to sound safer
- using more technical language than the situation needs

## How a senior thinks

A senior engineer speaks to align, not to perform:

> I want to make clear what changed, what still matters, and what could block the decision.

That intent usually improves the communication by itself.

## What the interviewer wants to see

Interviewers usually want to know:

- you can structure the answer
- you make the reasoning easy to follow
- you can balance clarity with brevity

That feels much more trustworthy than decorative language.

> Good communication does not decorate understanding. It shortens the path to it.

> If the other person still has to guess your point, the answer is not there yet.
